Online Manuscript Submission
Springer now offers authors, editors and reviewers of Machine Translation the option of using our fully web-enabled online manuscript submission and review system. To keep the review time as short as possible (no postal delays!), we encourage authors to submit manuscripts online to the journal‘s editorial office. Our online manuscript submission and review system offers authors the option to track the progress of the review process of manuscripts in real time. Manuscripts should be submitted to:

http://coat.edmgr.com

The online manuscript submission and review system for Machine Translation offers easy and straightforward log-in and submission procedures. This system supports a wide range of submission file formats: for manuscripts - Word, WordPerfect, RTF, TXT and LaTex; for figures - TIFF, GIF, JPEG, EPS, PPT, and Postscript.

NOTE: By using the online manuscript submission and review system, it is NOT necessary to submit the manuscript also in printout + disk. In case you encounter any difficulties while submitting your manuscript on line, please get in touch with the responsible Editorial Assistant by clicking on “CONTACT US” from the tool bar.

http://coat.edmgr.com
Electronic figures
Electronic versions of your figures must be supplied. For vector graphics, EPS is the preferred format. For bitmapped graphics, TIFF is the preferred format. The following resolutions are optimal: line figures - 600 - 1200 dpi; photographs - 300 dpi; screen dumps - leave as is. Colour figures can be submitted in the RGB colour system. Font-related problems can be avoided by using standard fonts such as Times Roman, Courier and Helvetica.
Colour figures
Colour figures may be printed at the author’s expense. Please indicate at submission which figures should be printed in colour, the number of colour pages you prefer and to which address we can send the invoice. In addition, please specify if figures are to appear together on a colour page.
Language
We appreciate any efforts that you make to ensure that the language is corrected before submission.
This will greatly improve the legibility of your paper if English is not your first language.
Manuscripts in LaTeX

Figures
All photographs, graphs and diagrams should be referred to as a 'Figure' and they should be numbered consecutively (1, 2, etc.). Multi-part figures ought to be labelled with lower case letters (a, b, etc.). Please insert keys and scale bars directly in the figures. Relatively small text and great variation in text sizes within figures should be avoided as figures are often reduced in size. Figures may be sized to fit approximately within the column(s) of the journal. Provide a detailed legend (without abbreviations) to each figure, refer to the figure in the text and note its approximate location in the margin. Please place the legends in the manuscript after the references.
Tables
Each table should be numbered consecutively (I, II, III, etc.). In tables, footnotes are preferable to long explanatory material in either the heading or body of the table. Such explanatory footnotes, identified by superscript letters, should be placed immediately below the table. Please provide a caption (without abbreviations) to each table, refer to the table in the text and note its approximate location in the margin. Finally, please place the tables after the figure legends in the manuscript.
Section Headings
Section headings should be numbered (e.g., 1., 1.1, 1.1.1, 2., 2.1, etc.) according to the following examples:
1. Exciting heading
1.1. ANOTHER EXCITING HEADING (Small caps)
1.3.1. These Headings Are Exciting
1.3.1.4. Try to avoid going down to this level if possible
Use LaTeX commands /section{Exciting heading} etc. to generate these styles automatically.
Appendices
Supplementary material which cannot be incorporated into the text should be collected in an Appendix and placed before the Notes and Reference sections.
Notes
The journal has endnotes rather than footnotes. Notes, which should be kept to a minimum, should be indicated by consecutive superscript numbers in the text and listed at the end of the article before the References. (Use the LaTeX command \endnote{...})
Punctuation and style
It is the journal‘s policy to publish in the variety of English most appropriate for the author(s) – usually American or British. Rules of spelling, capitalization and punctuation will follow accordingly.
Double quotes are used for quotations and for the introduction of new terms ("scare quotes"). Single quotes are used for titles of articles and for quotes within quotes, but primarily for glosses and translations when they occur in the text, as well as for translations following indented examples.
Use abbreviations sparingly, unless they are so common as to be unlikely to be misunderstood by the readership of this journal (USA, MT, NP, LFG). Always explain them the first time they are used, and use them consistently thereafter. Assume that abbreviations will be read as acronyms (e.g. "an MT system", not "a MT system"). Do not introduce abbreviations if the term is not going to be reused.
Where punctuation marks occur adjacent to a quotation mark, they follow the quotation mark unless they are part of the quoted material, e.g. I know he did not say "is it grammatical?"; but did he say "it is grammatical"?
Where a raised endnote reference number occurs adjacent to a punctuation mark, it always occurs after the punctuation.
Example Sentences and Cited Forms
In the text, cited forms which consist of a word or SHORT phrase should appear within the text in italics.
Non-English citations should be accompanied by a gloss in single quotes. For example, "the English phrase kick the bucket might be rendered in French as casser sa pipe ‘break one‘s pipe‘." For languages which use a non-Roman writing system, (normally) give the citation in standard orthography along with a transcription in italics followed by a gloss, e.g. [CYRILLIC HERE] perevod ‘translation‘. For transcriptions/transliterations follow the accepted norm. For Japanese, use the phonetic (Hepburn) system rather than phonemic or "waapuroo" transcription. So, fuji not huzi, joohoo not zyouhou. Katakana words should also be transliterated: shisutemu not system. For Chinese, use pinyin; normally, tone diacritics can be omitted, unless needed to disambiguate, in which case use accents rather than superscript numbers. It is appreciated if authors can use standard LaTeX packages (and indicate to us
which they have used) for non-Roman scripts.
Longer examples should be separated from the text, numbered with arabic numerals enclosed in parentheses and referred to by this number. We prefer the use of the LaTeX packages ling.sty which numbers and lays out examples automatically. Always refer to the example by number, even if it appears in the text immediately. Say "as shown in (5)", not "as shown here", or "as follows".
If several examples are cited together as a group, use a numeral enclosed in parentheses for the whole group, and a lowercase letter of the alphabet followed by a period for each example. Align the first words of all examples (excluding diacritics for grammaticality status) with one another. In the text, examples and subexamples are referred to by their numbers and letters enclosed in parentheses, as in (5), (7a), (8b), etc.
Numbered examples in English should be set in normal type. Examples in languages other than English must be translated into English twice. First give a word-for-word gloss, and then an idiomatic translation.
The word-for-word glosses should be neatly aligned under the original forms, and the idiomatic translation follows on a line below, enclosed in single quotes. The foreign language text will be set in italics, or - for languages using non-Roman script - in the original orthography it this is possible and appropriate. In this case, it should be accompanied by a phonetic transcription as well as a literal gloss.
In any case, the literal gloss will be set in small capitals, except for grammar glosses like subj, obj. pres, and so on. Abbreviations should be explained in an endnote if they are not obvious. The idiomatic translation will appear in single qoutes.
Examples in other languages should be in italics (or in standard orthography) accompanied by an aligned LITERAL gloss (use small capitals for the words and normal case for grammatical indications) as well as a free translation. If both are (generally) identical, the literal gloss can be omitted. Glosses may be omitted in special circumstances (e.g. ungrammatical examples), but this should be explained in an endnote.
In endnotes, examples are numbered using lowercase Roman numerals: (i), (ii), (iiia), etc.
Permissions
It is the responsibility of the author to obtain written permission for quotation from unpublished material, or for all quotations in excess of 250 words in one extract or 500 words in total from any work still in copyright, and for the reprinting of illustrations or tables from unpublished or copyrighted material.
Trademarks should be acknowledged. It may be appropriate to cover this with a generic statement "All trademarks are hereby acknowledged" near the beginning of the text.
Proofs
The author will receive two sets of page proofs of the article from the setter (with an annotated copy of the manuscript), and is requested to return one, corrected (with the manuscript), to the publisher within three days of receipt. In the case of proofs not being returned in time, they may be read by the editor against a second copy of the manuscript and passed for publication without the author‘s comment.
Offprints
Twenty-five offprints of each article will be provided free of charge. Additional offprints can be ordered when the proofs are returned to the publisher.
Copyright
Copyright will be established in the name of Springer.
